## Key Stakeholders

### 1. International Banking Leadership

**Characteristics:**

* Supports transformation
* More aligned with structural solutions
* Politically aware

**Behavior Patterns:**

* Uses influence rather than direct confrontation
* Brings external support (resources, people)
* Can shift the local power balance by validating structural changes that are difficult to push only from the local role
* May create execution pressure inside its own team before full local context is understood

**Implication:**

* Strong ally for structural initiatives
* Requires clear articulation of impact and progress
* Alignment with International Banking must include sequencing guardrails, not only strategic agreement

---

### 2. Local CEO

**Characteristics:**

* High pressure for visible results
* Focus on speed and optics
* Low tolerance for complexity

**Behavior Patterns:**

* Public escalation of issues
* Preference for quick evidence of progress
* Tendency to bypass formal structures
* Reinterprets outcomes post-facto to validate prior pressure
* Personalizes accountability when outcomes challenge prior positioning

**Implication:**

* Requires simplified narratives
* Needs visible progress signals
* Avoid exposing internal complexity directly
* Attribution must be proactively managed before and after delivery

---

### 3. Business Stakeholders

**Characteristics:**

* Outcome-focused
* Limited visibility into technical constraints

**Behavior Patterns:**

* Question delays and priorities
* Expect predictable delivery

**Implication:**

* Must improve transparency and communication

---

### 4. Internal Technology Leaders

**Characteristics:**

* Capability and political capital vary significantly across teams
* Some leaders are strong structural operators but carry political wear
* Some leaders are operationally reliable but not yet strategic enough
* Regionalized leaders may have institutional knowledge but limited local authority

**Behavior Patterns:**

* Leaders with business-facing friction may accumulate resistance from Sebastián
* Politically cautious leaders may avoid necessary friction with other areas
* Newly introduced trusted leaders need visible evidence of control before their authority is accepted at scale

**Implication:**

* Leadership moves require both capability assessment and political carrying-capacity assessment
* Trusted operators may need narrative protection, not just delegation
* Reset roles should be framed as structural capability upgrades, not personal corrections

## Operating Rules

### 1. Control the Narrative

* Translate complex realities into clear messages
* Provide visible progress
* Avoid exposing raw system constraints
* Secure attribution as part of the narrative, not after the fact

---

### 2. Align Before Execution

* Pre-align key stakeholders before major actions
* Reduce surprise and resistance
* Confirm whether executive asks are directional mandates or immediate execution requests before mobilizing teams

---

### 3. Show Evidence, Not Intent

* Demonstrate progress through concrete outputs
* Avoid abstract plans without proof

---

### 4. Attribution vs Execution Ownership

* Successful outcomes trigger **post-hoc narrative competition**
* Stakeholders attempt to align results with their prior stance

**Implication:**

* Execution alone does not secure recognition
* Attribution must be actively shaped and reinforced

---

### 5. Separate Internal Truth from External Communication

* Internal system: full accuracy
* External system: simplified and aligned

---

### 6. Use Structure as Protection

* Clear frameworks and plans reduce arbitrary pressure

---

### 7. Pre-Align Critical Escalation Paths

* Anticipate escalation from high-pressure stakeholders
* Align with higher authority (e.g., International Banking) before conflict materializes

**Implication:**

* Escalation becomes a validation mechanism, not a risk
* Reduces probability of forced suboptimal decisions

---

### 8. Use International Banking as Cover, Not Substitution

* International Banking alignment can provide political cover for structural transformation.
* Political cover does not replace local sequencing, impact assessment, or continuity planning.
* When International Banking teams push for fast artifacts without context, clarify the actual executive intent before accepting the timeline.

**Implication:**

* Use higher-level alignment to protect the direction.
* Use local operating judgment to control the pace and method.

---

## Tactical Patterns

### 2. Visibility

### 3. Escalation Management

### 5. Attribution Control

* Reinforce ownership of outcomes during:

  * Execution updates
  * Milestone communications
  * Final delivery narratives

* Use:

  * Written communication
  * Executive forums
  * Third-party validation (e.g., International Banking)

**Goal:**

* Prevent narrative drift after successful delivery

---

### 6. Channel Discipline Under Pressure

* High-pressure stakeholders may bypass formal channels when a new structure threatens informal influence.
* The response should redirect consistently and make the official channel more useful than the informal one.
* Visible structure and business-readable process artifacts can turn resistance into validation.

---

### 7. Stability-Based Resistance Reduction

* Local resistance can soften when the environment receives stronger stability, visibility, and executive alignment signals.
* A previously conflict-oriented stakeholder may become more open when the transformation appears controlled instead of experimental.
* This shift should be treated as a temporary operating window until reinforced by repeated evidence.

## Executive Pressure Patterns (Observed)

### Problem Aggregation Under Pressure

* Multiple unrelated issues are presented as a single problem
* Forces immediate simplification and response

**Implication:**

* Must separate problems quickly
* Avoid accepting incorrect aggregation

---

### Sarcasm as Escalation Mechanism

* Use of rhetorical or ironic statements (e.g., “architecture from the 70s”)

**Implication:**

* Not a technical critique → it is a pressure signal
* Response must reframe to facts without confrontation

---

### Channel Bypass

* Use of informal channels (e.g., WhatsApp) for escalation

**Implication:**

* Requires immediate but controlled response
* Narrative must be concise and high-signal

---

### Context-Blind Acceleration

* Higher-level teams may convert a directional executive target into an immediate artifact request.
* Example pattern: asking for a large reduction list before the local impact, sequencing, and continuity analysis is complete.

**Implication:**

* Clarify executive intent directly with the decision owner.
* Separate strategic agreement from execution timing.
* Avoid allowing urgency translation errors to become operating commitments.

## Failure Signals

If observed:

* Increasing public escalations
* Misalignment between stakeholders
* Pressure overriding structure
* Confusion about priorities
* Rewriting of outcomes that disconnect execution from ownership
* Targeting of individuals post-conflict (e.g., removal pressure)

→ Political environment is degrading
